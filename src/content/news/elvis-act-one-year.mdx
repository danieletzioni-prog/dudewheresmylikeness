Tennessee's ELVIS Act was the first US statute to explicitly include **voice** as
a protected attribute under right of publicity, and the first to name AI digital
replicas directly. A year later, the interesting question isn't "is it working?" —
it's "what does working look like?"

## The theory of the law

The Nashville music industry, which drove the bill, wasn't primarily worried about
a single headline-grabbing deepfake. They were worried about a long tail of low-grade
cloning — session singer voices scraped from stems, guide vocals cloned for scratch
tracks, demo beds generated from dead artists' catalogs. The ELVIS Act was drafted
to give rights-holders a clean cause of action for that tail.

## What we've seen

Most of the first year was takedown letters and licensing negotiations — not
litigation. That's what you'd expect from a well-drafted statute in a concentrated
industry: the fight moves upstream, to contract terms and platform policies. The
label-side attorneys I've talked to describe the act as a "drafting aid" more than
a "weapon" — it gives them something specific to point to when negotiating with AI
vendors.

## Why this matters for other states

A half-dozen states have introduced ELVIS-style bills. The lesson from Tennessee
isn't that the statute produces visible wins; it's that the statute **reshapes
private negotiation.** Any state evaluating similar legislation should be asking
about the industry structure in their jurisdiction, not just the legal text.
